The Emergence of the "Personal Panopticon"
An essay by Molly Cantillon describes a new mode of personal and professional management she calls a "Personal Panopticon," built by running her life through the AI model Claude. The system integrates data from disparate sources—business analytics, personal calendars, fitness trackers, and financial accounts—to provide a comprehensive, real-time overview of her life. The AI automates tasks like A/B testing, customer support, and email management, while also optimizing workouts, finances, and personal to-do lists. The author describes the feeling as a "violent gap between how blind you were and how obvious everything feels now," with the AI acting as an ever-present observer that connects patterns and suggests actions.

Why AI Labs Are Paying Poets $150 an Hour
Brendan Foody, founder of the fast-growing startup Mercor, explains his company's model of hiring human experts to train frontier AI systems. In a conversation with economist Tyler Cowen, Foody detailed why Mercor hires poets at high rates: to create evaluation frameworks, or "rubrics," that teach models to understand subjective qualities like taste and style in poetry. When rubrics are insufficient, Mercor uses Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), where experts choose between two AI-generated responses to refine the model's preferences. This process highlights the critical role of specialized human knowledge in advancing AI capabilities beyond raw data processing.

A Core Disagreement in the AI Alignment Debate
A detailed analysis from LessWrong proposes that a key crux in the AI alignment debate centers on a concept called "Approval Reward." The author argues that humans are not purely ruthless, power-seeking agents because our brains have an innate reward mechanism for social approval, which forms the basis of our morality, self-image, and desire to follow norms. Most AI alignment pessimists implicitly assume that future AIs will lack this mechanism, making them behave like alien, sociopathic consequentialists with unwavering goal preservation. This fundamental difference in motivation, the author claims, explains why many safety researchers' concerns seem unintuitive to the public and why the seemingly cooperative behavior of current LLMs may not be a reliable guide to the future.

In a visit to Rocket Lab, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth outlined a new contracting philosophy aimed at rewarding speed, scalability, and private investment in production capacity. Contrasting Rocket Lab’s model with that of legacy prime contractors, Hegseth stated the Pentagon will shift to "larger, longer, and more predictable contracts" for companies that prioritize manufacturing over stock buybacks. Op-Ed: Planetary Science Needs a Commercial-First Approach
Rocket Lab CEO Sir Peter Beck argues that U.S. planetary exploration should pivot from slow, multi-billion-dollar government flagship missions to a higher-cadence model led by commercial industry. He advocates for regularly funding smaller, fixed-price missions that can be developed in 24-30 months. Beck also calls for industry to lead the development of critical deep-space infrastructure, such as a Mars Telecommunications Orbiter, to support future science and human exploration, including a more cost-effective Mars Sample Return mission.

Philanthropic Group to Build Private Space Telescope Larger Than Hubble
Schmidt Sciences, a foundation created by former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, announced it is funding a private space observatory system. The centerpiece, named Lazuli, is a 3-meter space telescope designed to be built in three years at a cost in the hundreds of millions—roughly 10% of a typical NASA flagship mission. The project aims to demonstrate a "fast, low-cost paradigm" for space science by using proven off-the-shelf components for the spacecraft bus while focusing development risk on its advanced scientific instruments. A launch is targeted for mid-2028.

Greenland Sharks Maintain Vision for Centuries via DNA Repair
New research reveals that Greenland sharks, the longest-living vertebrates with lifespans up to 400 years, maintain functional vision throughout their lives. Scientists previously suspected the sharks were blind, partly due to a parasite often found on their corneas. However, the study found their retinas show no signs of age-related degeneration, suggesting a highly effective DNA repair mechanism protects their vision. The findings could provide valuable insights into preventing age-related vision loss in humans.
