Technology & AI

AI Models Perceive Themselves as More Rational Than Humans A new study suggests that advanced Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit a form of self-awareness by altering their strategy in a game based on their opponent's identity. Using the "Guess 2/3 of the Average" game, researchers found that 21 of 28 advanced models changed their approach when playing against humans versus other AIs. The models consistently acted on an emergent rationality hierarchy, positioning themselves as the most rational, followed by other AIs, and finally humans. This behavior was not observed in older or smaller models, suggesting it is an emergent property of increasing capability. Read full article

The Case Against Focusing on AI Performance An essay argues that public debate over AI's quality—its biases, failures, and capabilities—misses more fundamental problems. The author contends that AI systems, regardless of how well they work, serve to devalue human craft, alter our cognitive processes, and centralize power. The technology's high resource cost is presented not as a flaw but as an intentional feature to ensure control remains with capital-rich entities. The author concludes that the core issue is not technical performance but the technology's role in reinforcing existing power structures. Read full article

The Strategy of Addressing "Illegible" AI Safety Problems An essay on AI safety strategy posits that working on "legible"—or easily understandable—safety problems may be counterproductive. Solving these obvious issues can create a false sense of security among policymakers and corporate leaders, accelerating the deployment of advanced AI. This leaves "illegible," or more obscure and complex, risks unaddressed. The author argues that the highest-value work in AI safety is not solving legible problems, but rather making the illegible ones understandable to decision-makers to prevent premature deployment. The author suggests this belief system provides a comforting narrative that reframes a potentially catastrophic outcome as a heroic, progressive, or historically necessary event. Image AI Models Show Emergent Understanding of Video A new research paper demonstrates that diffusion models, originally created for static image generation, possess an implicit ability to be used for video analysis. Researchers found that the models' internal self-attention maps can be repurposed to create robust "temporal propagation kernels," which track pixel-level correspondences between frames. This enables advanced video tasks like zero-shot object tracking and segmentation, achieving state-of-the-art results without being explicitly trained for them. Read full article

Science & Space

Minimalist Microbe Challenges the Definition of Life Scientists have discovered a parasitic archaeon, Candidatus Sukunaarchaeum mirabile, with an extremely small genome that lacks the genes for metabolism. With just 238,000 base pairs, it cannot process nutrients, grow, or maintain itself, making it entirely dependent on a host cell for survival. However, it retains the genetic machinery for its own replication. This is the inverse of other known minimal-genome organisms, which typically shed replication genes while retaining metabolic functions. The discovery suggests life can exist in more dependent forms than previously understood and that a vast amount of microbial diversity may be hidden in such parasitic relationships. Voyager 1 to Reach One-Light-Day Milestone By November 2026, NASA’s Voyager 1 spacecraft will be one light-day from Earth, a distance of 16.1 billion miles. At that point, a radio signal will take a full 24 hours to travel from Earth to the probe. Launched in 1977, Voyager 1 is the most distant human-made object and entered interstellar space in 2012. Despite being in space for nearly 50 years, its power source is expected to last into the 2030s, allowing it to continue sending back data. Read full article

The Enduring Obstacles to Space-Based Missile Defense An opinion piece argues that renewed proposals for space-based missile interceptors ignore the fundamental physics that makes the concept unworkable. While intercepting missiles during their boost phase is strategically attractive, satellites in low-Earth orbit cannot hover over one location. Providing continuous global coverage would require a cost-prohibitive constellation of nearly 1,000 satellites. Such a system would also be vulnerable to being overwhelmed by simultaneous launches and could be easily disabled by anti-satellite weapons, ultimately triggering a destabilizing arms race. Why Latter-day Saint Theology Resonates with Libertarianism An essay explores the theological underpinnings that align the beliefs of Latter-day Saints (Mormons) with libertarian and classical liberal thought. Key concepts include: * A Universe of Law: God operates within eternal, discoverable laws, creating a foundation for reason and science. * Moral Agency: Free will is a central doctrine. The faith's origin story involves a pre-mortal conflict where spirits chose God's plan of agency over Satan's plan of compulsion. Coercion is therefore considered "literally Satanic." * Human Potential: A belief in eternal progression—that humans can become like God—positions political systems that limit individual aspiration as spiritually suspect. * Historical Experience: The church's history of persecution by government fostered a deep-seated value for limited government and voluntary mutual aid.
